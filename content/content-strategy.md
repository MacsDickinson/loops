# Content Strategy: Software Delivery in the Age of AI

**Created**: March 7, 2026
**For**: Engineering Leadership Thought Leadership
**Constraint**: Focus on the problem space, not product/solution

---

## Executive Summary

Position as the voice on how AI acceleration is reshaping software delivery bottlenecks. The narrative: AI isn't replacing developers—it's shifting where engineering leaders need to focus their attention from build to discovery, operationalisation, and growth.

**Core thesis**: Every improvement in software delivery has been about tightening feedback loops. AI dramatically tightens the build loop, which exposes weaknesses in the other loops that have been masked by slow build times.

## Core Narrative Themes

### Theme 1: The Bottleneck Migration
**The Problem**: As AI compresses build time, bottlenecks migrate upstream (discovery) and downstream (operationalisation, learning).

**Why this matters**:
- Cost of being wrong drops (fast builds mean cheap experiments)
- Cost of being unclear stays the same (AI builds wrong things quickly)
- New value creation zones emerge in discovery and production learning

**Content angles**:
- "When build gets 10x faster, what becomes the new bottleneck?"
- "Why AI coding agents keep building the wrong thing"
- "The discovery tax: what happens when requirements are unclear"
- Data point: METR research showing AI task completion acceleration

### Theme 2: Feedback Loops as Universal Language
**The Problem**: Teams are adopting AI tools piecemeal without rethinking their delivery process.

**Why this matters**:
- Agile, DevOps, Lean, XP all work by tightening feedback loops
- AI is just another loop-tightening technology
- The principles don't change, but the application does

**Content angles**:
- "Every delivery innovation is a feedback loop story"
- "What Agile got right about AI (and doesn't need to change)"
- "From DevOps to AIDevOps: what stays, what shifts"
- Historical parallel: How DevOps emerged when cloud made infrastructure programmable

### Theme 3: The Discovery Gap
**The Problem**: Most AI coding tool adoption focuses on implementation, ignoring the requirements clarity problem.

**Why this matters**:
- Copilot/Cursor/etc. solve "write the code faster"
- But if the spec is ambiguous, you just get wrong code faster
- Discovery (requirements → testable spec) is the most underserved part of AI-assisted delivery

**Content angles**:
- "The missing piece in AI-assisted development"
- "Why your team's AI productivity isn't showing up in velocity"
- "From idea to spec: the conversation engineering teams aren't having"
- "BDD and AI: why acceptance criteria matter more, not less"

### Theme 4: Production as the New Frontier
**The Problem**: When build is cheap, the bar for "what ships" drops, creating downstream chaos.

**Why this matters**:
- Cheap prototypes create expensive expectations
- Production-ready ≠ works in demo
- Need explicit lifecycle stages (Pilot vs. Production)

**Content angles**:
- "The prototype trap: when moving fast breaks things later"
- "What does 'production-ready' mean when AI writes the code?"
- "Continuous compliance: DevOps for regulatory requirements"
- "From prototype to production: the missing graduation path"

### Theme 5: Role Evolution, Not Role Replacement
**The Problem**: "Will AI replace [PMs/QA/engineers]?" is the wrong question.

**Why this matters**:
- Roles evolve as build gets faster
- PMs can get closer to implementation
- Engineers spend more time on architecture, less on mechanics
- QA shifts from manual testing to test strategy

**Content angles**:
- "What engineering leaders do when AI does the coding"
- "The PM-engineer convergence: why it's happening now"
- "From writing code to architecting systems: the engineering career path shift"
- "QA in the age of AI: from testers to test strategists"

## Voice & Style Guidelines

### Do:
- Use data and specific examples
- Acknowledge established practices (Agile, DevOps, etc.) as foundation
- Ask provocative questions
- Share honest observations (including what doesn't work)
- Use clear, direct language
- Reference respected sources (METR, research, practitioner writing)

### Don't:
- Hype AI as magical
- Dismiss existing practices
- Claim everything is changing
- Use buzzwords without definition
- Make it about selling a product
- Speak in absolutes

### Tone:
- **Experienced**: This is based on observation, not theory
- **Pragmatic**: Focused on what works, not what's cool
- **Curious**: Still figuring it out, not prescriptive
- **Inclusive**: "We're all navigating this" not "I have the answers"

## Risk Mitigation

### Risk: "Everyone's talking about AI + dev, saturated space"
**Mitigation**: Angle is different. Not "AI can code" but "when AI codes, where should leaders focus?" Meta-level, strategy-focused, grounded in delivery fundamentals.

### Risk: "Takes focus away from building the product"
**Mitigation**: Content production is 4-6 hours/week max. LinkedIn posts are fast. Substack draws from work already done (internal docs). This is marketing disguised as thought leadership.

### Risk: "Competitors see our thinking"
**Mitigation**: By the time competitors understand the problem, we're already building the solution. Being first to articulate the problem = category leadership.

### Risk: "Audience tunes out if no solution offered"
**Mitigation**: Each piece offers actionable insight, even without product. "Ask these questions in your next planning meeting" or "Here's how to identify discovery bottlenecks."

## Appendix: Example Post Outlines

### Example LinkedIn Post 1: "The Bottleneck Migration"
```
I've been watching engineering teams adopt AI coding tools over the past year.

One pattern keeps showing up: velocity spikes for 2-3 sprints, then plateaus.

The bottleneck moved.

When AI compresses build time, teams hit a new constraint:
requirements clarity.

Copilot can write the code in 10 minutes.
But if the spec is ambiguous, you just get the wrong code in 10 minutes.

The cost of being wrong dropped.
The cost of being unclear didn't.

Teams that figure out how to tighten the discovery loop
(idea → clear spec) see sustained velocity gains.

Teams that don't? Back to normal after the honeymoon period.

Engineering leaders: where are you seeing this play out?
```

### Example LinkedIn Post 2: "What Agile Got Right"
```
Hot take: AI doesn't invalidate Agile. It validates it.

Every innovation in software delivery has been about
tightening feedback loops:

- Agile: shorter cycles between plan and release
- DevOps: faster loop from code to production
- Lean: tighter loop from idea to validation
- XP: "if feedback is good, more feedback is better"

AI is just the latest loop-tightening technology.

What changes: WHERE the loop tightens (build phase).
What doesn't: the underlying principle (fast feedback beats slow feedback).

The teams struggling with AI tools?
They're trying to bolt AI onto waterfall processes.

The teams winning?
They already thought in feedback loops.
AI just makes them faster.

If your delivery process didn't work before AI,
making it faster won't fix it.
```

### Example Substack Outline: "The Discovery Gap"
**Opening**: Story of a team that adopted Cursor, saw huge productivity spike, then shipped a feature that missed the mark entirely. Built fast, built wrong.

**Thesis**: AI coding tools solve implementation speed. They don't solve requirements clarity. Discovery (idea → testable spec) is now the bottleneck.

**The problem in detail**:
- PMs describe features in natural language
- Engineers interpret and build
- Gap between intent and implementation
- Rework cycle is expensive even when build is fast

**Why this is getting worse**:
- Faster build = more things built per sprint
- More output = more surface area for misunderstanding
- AI agents are literal, not intuitive

**What good discovery looks like**:
- Clear acceptance criteria (BDD-style)
- Edge cases identified upfront
- Security/compliance concerns surfaced early
- Structured dialogue, not just "build this"

**Historical parallel**:
DevOps emerged when infrastructure became programmable.
Discovery needs similar evolution now that code generation is commodified.

**Actionable close**:
- Audit your last 3 features: where did requirements fall short?
- Ask: "Do we spend more time clarifying after build starts or before?"
- Experiment: Write acceptance tests before writing the spec

**CTA**: "What's your team's biggest discovery bottleneck? Reply and let's compare notes."
